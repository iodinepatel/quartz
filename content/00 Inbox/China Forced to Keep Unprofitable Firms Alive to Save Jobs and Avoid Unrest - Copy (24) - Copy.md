Saddled with the most loss-making industrial companies since 2001, Xi Jinping is trying to clean up his economy without triggering mass layoffs.

On the factory floor of a 69-year-old liquor plant in northwestern China, a dozen workers toil away sticking labels by hand on bottles of a fiery liquor known as baijiu, the nation’s most popular spirit.

The inefficiency on display at [Shaanxi Qinyang Changsheng Brewing Co.](http://www.qinyangjy.com/about.html "Click to view webpage.") — a firm that hasn’t turned a profit since 2020 — might seem like an anomaly in a country where robots are [replacing](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-07-31/china-s-robotaxi-dreams-sparks-anxiety-over-losing-jobs-to-ai) manual labor. But it’s a scene replicated across the nation, as company owners and local officials go to great lengths to protect jobs and keep struggling firms alive.

“If we shut down, our workers will lose their income and won’t be able to receive a pension,” said Megan Xiao, 35, daughter of the company’s chairman who helps with its marketing. The factory employs 300 workers including some under a government poverty alleviation program. “Closing would cause huge social problems for our area,” she added.

In the 1990s, policymakers set the stage for China’s rapid rise to become the world’s top manufacturer by shuttering thousands of unproductive companies and laying off [millions](https://www.newsweek.com/zhu-rongjis-promise-146327 "Click to view webpage.") of workers — tough measures that helped improve efficiency across the board. But as China’s $19 trillion economy slows, demand for many products is now saturated, and shoppers rattled by a yearslong property crisis are becoming more thrifty, even as prices fall.

The new reality has saddled China with the most loss-making industrial companies since 2001, the tail end of that earlier period of reform. Local leaders laden in debt are rolling out tax breaks and subsidies for companies, in a bid to stave off the double whammy of job and revenue losses.

That’s left President Xi Jinping’s government grappling with two conflicting goals: Avoiding mass unemployment that could trigger social unrest, while also closing enterprises churning out excess goods that are spurring deflation at home and a wave of anti-dumping tariffs from Europe to Latin America, and the US.

For China’s top leaders, employment is an even more politically sensitive issue than economic growth, according to [Neil Thomas](https://asiasociety.org/policy-institute/neil-thomas), a fellow for Chinese politics at the Asia Society Policy Institute’s Center for China Analysis.

“Unemployed individuals are seen as having less to lose from protesting and so pose a greater risk,” he said. “Central efforts to cut industrial capacity are therefore likely to encounter resistance — not only from employees and managers of affected enterprises, but local officials whose performance evaluations depend on maintaining social stability.”

Xi’s tariff showdown with Donald Trump is only intensifying the problem. The trade war has slashed exports to the US and imperiled millions of Chinese jobs, even after a truce brought Washington’s additional levies down to 30% from 145%. Manufacturing accounts for one fifth of China’s workforce, according to Deutsche Bank AG — more than double the share in the US.

While Xi’s policy to “never kneel” before America’s most punishing tariff regime in a century has [captured](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-04-28/how-trump-s-china-tariffs-attacks-benefit-xi-as-nationalism-grows "Trump's China Attacks Unleash Wave of Nationalist Support for Xi") public support in China, layoffs could quickly sour that sentiment. Nationwide protests that prompted Xi to abruptly end the Covid Zero policy in late 2022 exemplified the Communist Party’s sensitivity to social unrest.

A protest against Covid-19 restrictions in Beijing in 2022.Source: Bloomberg

Already there are signs the [weakening](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-09/china-private-wage-data-goes-dark-with-jobs-at-risk-from-tariffs "China Wage Data Goes Missing as Tariffs Imperil Millions of Jobs") labor market is becoming a touchy subject: One of China’s largest online recruitment platforms Zhaopin Ltd. this year quietly stopped providing wage data it’s compiled for at least a decade.

Chinese policymakers have struggled for decades with the problem of wasteful production.

Premier Zhu Rongji in the late 1990s took the first swing at solving the issue, launching a campaign to close inefficient state-owned enterprises that were sucking resources from the economy through cheap funds and subsidies. That shrinking of the state came as Beijing unleashed a barrage of market-friendly reforms that enticed a surge of foreign investment and propelled a decade of double-digit growth, diluting the pain of mass layoffs.

Shortly after Xi came to power, top leaders returned to that playbook as they aimed to ease excessive output of steel and other commodities, amid global complaints of dumping.

This time around, however, the industries in need of intervention are mostly emerging in fields where private firms play a leading role, giving central authorities less control on who exits the market. Competition with the US has also elevated national security to a top priority, shrinking Beijing’s tolerance for anything that creates instability.

Still, Xi and his lieutenants recognize the overcapacity problem needs to be addressed. As China braced for a trade war with Trump, the president and premier in March both pledged to address “involution-style competition” among industries. That Internet buzzword describes a destructive state of intense competition sparked by excess capacity that forces companies and workers to overwork despite diminishing returns.

But without overhauling the incentive structure at various levels of China’s government, inertia among local officials could be hard to break. Chinese bureaucrats are primarily assessed on their region’s economic growth, and attracting investment is one of the easiest ways to boost figures*.* They also get to collect value-added taxes paid by producers. In a bid to combat the problem, China’s cabinet-like State Council introduced [rules](https://www.gov.cn/zhengce/content/202406/content_6957049.htm "Click to view webpage.") last year banning localities from providing preferential tax breaks or subsidies without higher approval.

Top officials have also urged lenders to end credit supply to [zombie](https://www.gov.cn/zhengce/2016-01/11/content_5031973.htm "Gov") companies — those kept alive by government subsidies or bank loans. While there are no official figures on how many zombie firms stalk China today, consultancy firm Kearney [estimates](https://www.kearney.com/documents/291362523/302259780/Zombie+buyers+beware%E2%80%93Regenerate-PDF.pdf) that in 2023 such firms saw a 27% surge in the Asian nation, although they only accounted for 3.4% of all companies.

Few regions illustrate the pitfalls as starkly as Shanxi, once a cradle of coal production in northwestern China where today thousands of abandoned mines litter the landscape. Almost 40% of industrial firms recorded a loss here last year — nearly double the national average.

Fixated on ending its dependence on Western technology, Xi’s government has been channeling investment away from infrastructure and real estate, prompting provinces like Shanxi to turn to [advanced manufacturing](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-07-15/evs-solar-tech-sectors-help-xi-jinping-s-china-navigate-property-slump "Xi’s Great Economic Rewiring Is Cushioning China’s Slowdown"), with a focus on the favored “new three” energy industries: solar, electric vehicles and lithium-ion batteries.

The plight of Dayun Automobile Co. is a case in point.

The Dayun production base in Yuncheng.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg

A closed dealership for Dayun’s Yuanhang brand in the city.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg

In its heyday, the truck manufacturer provided thousands of the highest-paying jobs in Yuncheng, an ancient city of 5 million people that’s a three-hour train ride from the baijiu factory. After sales peaked in 2017, Dayun — like many automakers around the nation — pivoted into the EV market, but struggled to sell premium sedans billed as [Chinese Bentleys.](https://x.com/mariocavolo/status/1794364592677941689 "video")

“This is the sad thing about China’s economy,” said Zhao Xinzheng, 47, who worked for Dayun as an autos salesman two decades ago, and now employs several of its former staffers at his chain of breakfast diners. “Once people discover a good industry, they swarm in and eat up the profits at lightning speed.”

Zhao Xinzheng Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg

“Shanxi isn’t even fit for manufacturing,” he adds. The province’s inland location makes it expensive to bring in components and send out cars, and it doesn’t have a competitive supply chain for parts. One advantage companies did enjoy, he said, was government support: Factories got financial rewards for setting up shop in Shanxi, while Dayun trucks were granted a highway toll exemption to bolster sales.

Dayun’s [prospectus](https://pdf.dfcfw.com/pdf/H2_AN202007021388978681_1.pdf?1648334693000.pdf "Click to view webpage.") from 2020 — when the firm attempted to stage an initial public offering to fund its EV expansion — provides a glimpse of its relationship with the authorities.

One third of the brand’s trucks were sold to a firm wholly [owned](https://www.sxycft.com/jybk/qcxs/ "Click to view webpage.") by Yuncheng’s local government in the three years before the attempted IPO, the prospectus shows, while tax benefits awarded for qualifying as a high-tech firm accounted for over 10% of its profits. In addition, Dayun received subsidies for EV sales between 2017 and 2018 equivalent to a fifth of its profits during those years, according to an official [notice](https://www.yuncheng.gov.cn/doc/2021/07/15/132118.shtml "Click to view webpage.").

Even that backing proved insufficient in China’s highly competitive car market, which has some 140 EV brands — less than [20 of which](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-07-11/less-than-20-chinese-ev-brands-to-be-profitable-by-decade-s-end "Click to view webpage.") are expected to be profitable by the end of the decade, according to consultancy [Alixpartners](https://www.alixpartners.com/ "Click to view webpage."). Following a lawsuit by suppliers for failing to make payments, Dayun in [November](https://www.dayunmotor.com/news-newsdetail-61-8823 "Click to view webpage.") began a court-ordered [restructuring](https://pccz.court.gov.cn/pcajxxw/pcgg/ggxq?id=C3ED1B43A9CE7BCB97CF548D12539F7C "Click to view webpage."), citing cashflow difficulties. But after downsizing about half of its staff, according to current and former workers, Dayun still resumed churning out trucks.

For the locals, Dayun’s collapse is unimaginable. Several residents interviewed by Bloomberg said they believe the government is actively working to resolve its issues and looking for investors.

“It’s impossible for Dayun to go bankrupt,” said Zhao, the diner owner. “The government would never allow it.”

Dayun and Yuncheng government didn’t respond to several requests for interviews.

Even if they want to, many failing companies in China simply aren’t able to wind down. Convincing a court to accept the case is often the first hurdle, according to [Wu Jian](https://www.zhonglunwende.com/en/index.php/Index/show/catid/3/id/96.html), senior partner at Zhonglun W&D Law Firm in Beijing, who specializes in bankruptcy. That’s because once a case enters the system, judges become personally accountable for maintaining social order, he said.

For sensitive cases, court officials are required to visit the homes of protestors and petitioners to resolve complaints, according to a 2021 legal [paper](https://lawreview.sjtu.edu.cn/ch/reader/create_pdf.aspx?file_no=20210105&flag=1&year_id=2021&quarter_id=1 "Click to view webpage.") citing internal court guidelines. Failing to prevent such incidents especially during major government events or holidays could lead to serious penalties such as being demoted.

“Defaults and bankruptcies are remarkably rare in China, compared to other economies,” said Louis Kuijs, chief Asia-Pacific economist at S&P Global Ratings. “While it’s tempting to resist firms going under, such an approach prevents a more efficient allocation of resources. Also, the lack of market discipline regarding weak firms causes problems for other, more healthy firms.”

China has made an effort to improve the bankruptcy system, as defaulted property developers grapple with some $150 billion of debt. That saw courts [triple](https://baijiahao.baidu.com/s?id=1693649460685139757&wfr=spider&for=pc "Click to view webpage.") their concluded case count last year from 2020. In part, that reflects the establishment of [specialized bankruptcy courts](https://www.chinacourt.org/article/detail/2023/01/id/7110002.shtml "Click to view webpage.") to limit local governments’ interference, although only about 100 exist across more than 3,000 courthouses throughout the country.

The front gate of Dayun’s production base in Yuncheng.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg

David Li Daokui, a regular adviser on policy in Beijing, says the solution lies with the central government. He suggests top officials impose a quota system restricting output for industries with overcapacity. That would give firms an incentive to exit, since they could sell their quotas to competitors, according to Li, who’s also an economics professor at Tsinghua University.

Back at the liquor factory, Chairman Xiao Yuxiang is hatching plans to keep his company alive. A line of non-alcoholic rice wine, catering to the more sober habits of Gen Z drinkers and new rules banning booze at government functions, is at the center of his strategy.

But he, too, would like to see more state intervention. Xiao proposes a system where companies are awarded licenses to produce liquor if they meet certain criteria for job creation and tax payment. That would curb excessive competition and allow makers with less contribution to exit, he said.

“So far we don’t see any determination from the government to solve the problem,” he said. “The economy’s improvement will probably depend on the timing and strength of such resolve.” — Colum Murphy
