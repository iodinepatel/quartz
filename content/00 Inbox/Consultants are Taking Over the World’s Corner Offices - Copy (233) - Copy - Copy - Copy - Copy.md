General Electric. Procter & Gamble. IBM.

For years, those companies and a handful of others were held aloft as “CEO Factories,” admired for their ability to recruit and mold corporate chiefs. Over a 20-year span, just three dozen companies produced [one-fifth](https://scholarcommons.scu.edu/finance/3/ "Link to research paper") of the chief executives in the entire S&P 1500 index.

Those factories, many of them sprawling global conglomerates, were leadership proving grounds where promising young recruits got shuttled from one challenging role to the next, and where running a business unit or geographic region inside of them was equal to steering a full-sized firm. The companies invested millions in training programs like GE’s [famed](https://www.jstor.org/stable/4164880 "Link to research paper") Crotonville campus, which schooled up-and-coming executives on leadership, finance and industry-specific skills. Boards preferred to hire CEOs from these leadership factories, and investors rewarded companies that did so, creating a virtuous C-suite cycle.

But just as GE’s Crotonville management school is no longer — the company [sold off](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2024-04-23/ge-s-ceo-factory-was-out-of-step-with-modern-leadership) the property in New York’s Hudson Valley in 2024 — the dominance of the traditional CEO factories is fast becoming a thing of the past. The companies most notably taking their place: consulting firms. Alumni of Accenture, Deloitte, PwC, EY and even little-known Swiss staffing firm The Adecco Group have all grabbed a bigger share of global CEO roles over the past 15 years, according to an exclusive analysis of the career paths of the CEOs at more than 4,300 global public companies. Meanwhile, the influence of storied CEO factories like GE and IBM has diminished, according to the analysis by Live Data Technologies.

The rise of a new type of CEO factory reflects new demands on CEOs. Businesses today increasingly sell services and “solutions,” putting a premium on fluency in digits and data. Globalized supply chains and markets require statesmanship to navigate a new wave of geopolitical storms. Corporate cultures have become more collaborative and purpose-driven, necessitating [social skills](https://hbr.org/2022/07/the-c-suite-skills-that-matter-most "The C-Suite Skills That Matter Most: Harvard Business Review") like listening, and the ability to communicate a vision. And technology keeps upending business models, with recent advancements in artificial intelligence underscoring the need for leaders who can anticipate disruptions. Soon, picking a CEO who doesn’t grok AI could be as dangerous as choosing one who didn’t grasp the Internet in 2000.

Leaders whose backgrounds fit the bill, increasingly, are coming from the ranks of professional services firms — and not just from McKinsey & Co., which has reliably churned out corporate chiefs for decades.

What’s behind this shift? One catalyst is the move by the Big Four accounting firms beyond auditing into strategy and execution. As they’ve expanded their scope, these firms have snatched up more of the best and brightest undergrads and MBAs. Once there, they’re trained in technical know-how, problem-solving and softer skills, while acquiring expertise across entire industries rather than at just one company. And though consultants have a reputation for parachuting in to deliver a buzzword-laden strategic vision, they also stick around to do the hard work of installing new technology and processes to improve their clients’ business.

“They are infiltrated everywhere inside corporations, and what they’re doing is much more hands-on — that’s good training for how companies are run,” says Tom Rodenhauser of Kennedy Intelligence, which covers the consulting sector. “You combine all those things, and they make good profiles for CEOs.”

While consultants tick a lot of boxes, they don’t always thrive in the C-suite. Just look at John Donahoe, the longtime Bain & Co. consultant who [flamed out](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-09-13/nike-nke-stock-upheaval-defines-ceo-john-donahoe-s-tenure) at Nike in 2024, or McKinsey alum Laxman Narasimhan, who was [ousted](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-08-13/starbucks-names-brian-niccol-as-ceo-replacing-laxman-narasimhan) by the board of Starbucks after just 17 months.

Still, the march of ex-consultants into the corner office continues. There’s [Rick Wurster](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/audio/2025-03-12/bloomberg-talks-charles-schwab-president-ceo-podcast), the McKinsey alum who just took the helm at Charles Schwab, while Omar Abbosh, installed last year as CEO at Pearson Plc, cut his teeth at Accenture. Companies are paying top dollar for them: Wurster’s compensation this year could [total](https://content.schwab.com/web/retail/public/about-schwab/schw_8k_093024.pdf "Link to SEC filing") $18 million, while Pearson [awarded](https://plc.pearson.com/sites/pearson-corp/files/pearson/annual-report-2024/governance-and-remuneration-2024.pdf "Link to remuneration report (.pdf)") Abbosh equity worth about $17 million. (Those hefty payouts, though, are more due to the cost of prying leaders out of their previous corporate jobs than the appeal of their consulting experience.)

A textbook example of the consultant-to-CEO metamorphosis is Sander van’t Noordende. Over the course of three decades at Accenture and its predecessor, Andersen Consulting, he separately ran two huge divisions that served clients in nearly every industry imaginable, from autos to chemicals, food to utilities. He honed his leadership skills by deploying specialist teams in both strategy and execution for projects like helping retailers increase their e-commerce sales. That hands-on experience prepared him well for his current job, CEO of Dutch talent giant Randstad NV, which generates more than $27 billion in annual revenue.

“The thing that Accenture is quite good at is, you have your strategy, now you need to put your money and even your people where your mouth is and come up with a new operating model,” van’t Noordende said in an interview. “Executives at Accenture have the same type of experience as a business-unit manager at Johnson & Johnson or at any other company.”

That experience is buttressed by rigorous training. Accenture [spends](https://www.accenture.com/content/dam/accenture/final/accenture-com/a-com-custom-component/iconic/document/Accenture-Fiscal-2024-Annual-Report.pdf#zoom=50 "Link to annual report from Accenture (.pdf)") more than $1 billion a year developing its 801,000 employees, the same amount GE [spent](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/podcast/knowledge-at-wharton-podcast/how-ge-builds-global-leaders-a-conversation-with-chief-learning-officer-susan-peters/ "How GE Builds Global Leaders: A Conversation with Chief Learning Officer Susan Peters | Knowledge at Wharton") back in its heyday. Programs include an international rotation that has has repositioned 10,000 executives over the past two years into fresh assignments.

“People can have multiple careers that you would otherwise have to leave to do,” said Angela Beatty, Accenture’s chief leadership and HR officer. “Not only do they advise on something, they deliver and implement it. It’s a way to practice and learn what it is like to lead a business under the umbrella of Accenture. And so it makes a lot of sense that someday they would become a CEO.”

A similar story is playing out with big banks, which also serve clients in a variety of industries, handle a widening array of key corporate functions (financing, M&A) and have fully [embraced](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/financial-services/our-insights/unlocking-value-from-technology-in-banking-an-investor-lens "Link to McKinsey report") emerging technologies. While Citigroup was the only bank in the latest CEO factory ranking’s top 10, the broader list also included HSBC (#13), Credit Suisse (#14) and UBS (#16).

Even firms like Deloitte and PwC, which traditionally just handled tax and compliance issues, are now playing a broader consultative role. PwC’s advisory business now generates [twice](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/about/transparency-report/2024/financials.html "Link to PwC annual report") as much revenue as its tax and legal services arm. That shift reflects the evolution of the CFO position, which has become much more [strategic](https://www.kornferry.com/institute/cfo-pulse-survey-2020 "Link to Korn/Ferry research") and influential inside the C-suite.

“We’ve grown our focus to more than just the CFO,” said PwC’s US chief commercial officer, [Kathryn Kaminsky](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/contacts/k/kathryn-kaminsky.html "Link to bio"). “We try to get people to be the most well-rounded they can be.” Former PwC partners who have gone on to be successful CEOs include Delta’s Ed Bastian and Richard Oldfield, [installed](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-09-10/schroders-names-finance-chief-as-ceo-ending-months-long-search?sref=QnKyEnuc) last year atop UK asset manager Schroders Plc. Berkshire Hathaway’s [new chief](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-03/buffett-s-exit-to-lift-energy-executive-abel-to-berkshire-ceo?sref=QnKyEnuc "Link to story"), Greg Abel, began his career as a PwC accountant.

The emergence of The Adecco Group among the leading factories, meanwhile, shows how workforce issues have lately garnered the spotlight, as businesses grappled with [remote work](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/audio/2023-09-11/companies-press-return-to-office-workers-want-to-wfh-podcast), the [“Great Resignation”](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-11-19/here-s-where-the-record-number-of-american-workers-are-quitting) and the impact of [AI on jobs](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-02-08/ai-is-driving-more-layoffs-than-companies-want-to-admit). “We deal with people — that’s our business,” said [Aly Sparks](https://www.linkedin.com/in/alysparks/ "Link to bio"), a senior HR and talent executive at the company, who has brought more rigor and structure to its leadership development programs. That’s helped groom executives like [Dominik de Daniel](https://home.kuehne-nagel.com/en/-/company/corporate-governance/member-dominik-de-daniel), CEO of Kühne Holding AG, the investment vehicle of German logistics tycoon Klaus-Michael Kühne.

That said, moving directly from a professional-services firm to the corner office is rare — there’s often an intermediate corporate role or two, like running the finance department, operations or strategy. That way, they can make “all the predictable mistakes” in that first post-consulting role, said [Elena Botelho](https://ghsmart.com/team/elena-lytkina-botelho/ "Link to bio"), a partner at ghSMART, a leadership advisory firm that works with corporate boards and investors.

“We always joke with our clients that you want that McKinsey person on their second job out of McKinsey,” said Botelho, whose analysis of what [sets successful CEOs apart](https://hbr.org/2017/05/what-sets-successful-ceos-apart "What Sets Successful CEOs Apart | Harvard Business Review") — dubbed the [CEO Genome Project](https://ceogenome.com/ "CEO Genome Project") — found that charisma, for example, mattered much less than traits like decisiveness and adaptability.

That’s where consultants have a leg up. “Their job is to adapt — to walk into a business and very quickly size it up,” she said. “Otherwise they couldn’t survive.” And in today’s volatile business climate, with uncertainty rampant and talk of a recession growing, corporate boards value that agility more than ever.

“Their leadership style is honed to today’s world,” said [Vikram Malhotra](https://www.mckinsey.com/our-people/vik-malhotra), a senior McKinsey leader who [analyzed](https://www.mckinsey.com/featured-insights/mckinsey-on-books/ceo-excellence "Link to book description") the career paths of 7,800 CEOs to identify patterns of success.

In contrast, the old-line CEO factories are less relevant today. One simple reason is they’re smaller. German industrial conglomerate Siemens topped the factory ranking for the first two periods studied (2009-2013 and 2014-2019), and its executives have gone on to run big firms like Alcoa, German steelmaker ThyssenKrupp and healthcare group Fresenius. But Siemens has fallen from the top spot now that it’s [spun off](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-05-08/now-you-see-it-now-you-don-t-the-great-dismantling-of-siemens) several large operating units like its power and healthcare divisions, which are big companies in their own right. Fellow conglomerate GE has also been [broken up](https://www.bloomberg.com/features/2024-ge-breakup/) into three separate companies.

But it’s not just because unwieldy conglomerates are out of fashion. Several of the best-known factories made a shambles of their own CEO succession plans, casting doubt on their ability to groom future leaders. In 2013 P&G put former CEO A.G. Lafley back in charge, replacing his hand-picked successor, Bob McDonald. A few years later GE had to install its [first-ever](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-11-11/how-ge-ceo-larry-culp-decided-to-split-conglomerate-into-three-companies?sref=QnKyEnuc) outsider CEO, Larry Culp, to rescue the company after a series of missteps by his predecessor, who lasted all of 14 months. Meanwhile, GE-bred CEOs contributed to the [crisis](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-03-25/boeing-ceo-calhoun-commercial-head-deal-to-step-down) now facing Boeing Co.

“Being a CEO is a three-act play,” said Kevin Sharer, the former CEO of Amgen (and a GE alum) who’s now an executive coach. “You have to get the job, do it well and then you have to find a successor who’s at least as good as you. If you blow act three, you live with it forever.”

Curiously absent among the new CEO factories are technology giants like Apple Inc., Amazon.com Inc. and Facebook parent Meta Platforms Inc., whose influence and stock-market returns have made them paragons of business over the past decade.

A few things keep them from being true CEO factories: One, when their leaders leave, they often turn to launching or investing in startups rather than running other large enterprises. (Intel’s new CEO, [Lip-Bu Tan](https://newsroom.intel.com/corporate/intel-appoints-lip-bu-tan-chief-executive-officer), is a longtime venture capitalist.) Two, the skill sets fostered in tech firms are geared less toward general management and more toward product development — think of Apple’s design guru Jony Ive. And some just haven’t been around long enough to compete with the big CEO factories (Meta only went public in 2012). There are also some high-profile flameouts, like Amazon veteran Dave Clark’s [abrupt departure](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-09-06/flexport-ceo-clark-resigns-says-founder-best-suited-to-lead) from Flexport Inc. after just one year at the helm.

One exception is Microsoft, which has moved up from 15th to 12th in the factory rankings, reflecting not only its storied and relatively longer history but also its longstanding focus on serving big organizations. Microsoft veterans who went on to run big companies include Kevin Johnson (Starbucks), Stephen Elop (Nokia) and David Risher (Lyft). IBM, which has provided consulting services since the early 1990s, is the only tech company to consistently place in the top 10 among CEO factories, although its ranking has been on the decline.

While Big Tech overall isn’t producing many CEOs for the outside world, digital expertise gleaned inside professional services firms has provided a boost to many executives’ careers. Another consultant-turned-CEO who has benefited from that shift is [Neville Roberts](https://www.planixs.com/about-us/), the CEO and co-founder of Planixs, which makes software to help banks manage their liquidity. Earlier in his career, asa partner at Accenture, he helped retailers like Best Buy integrate technology into their operations. He later became chief information officer of Best Buy before co-founding Planixs in 2011.

“It was the perfect training ground for me,” Roberts said of his time at Accenture. “You had to sell work, win work and deliver work. It’s like being a mini-CEO.”

A more recent example is [Matthew Fitzpatrick](https://www.linkedin.com/in/matthew-fitzpatrick-97b62722/ "Link to bio"), the [new CEO](https://www.invisible.co/blog/invisible-technologies-appoints-matt-fitzpatrick-chief-executive-officer#:~:text=%E2%80%8DSan%20Francisco%20%2D%20January%2021%2C%202025,with%20decades%20of%20leadership%20experience. "Link to announcement") of AI data-training provider Invisible Technologies, who previously ran McKinsey’s AI hub, QuantumBlack Labs. At McKinsey, he said, “people thought I was making PowerPoint slides,” rather than his actual job leading 1,000 engineers and developers who created and deployed generative AI for companies like ING Bank.

“If you think of the function of a CEO, a lot of it is strategy but it’s also thinking about how to be more efficient, and the biggest tool for that is digital,” Fitzpatrick said. “Consulting firms have been successful because they help companies make stuff work.”

Fitzpatrick illustrates McKinsey’s status as a C-suite training ground — it’s number one in our ranking, and among its 65,000 living alums there are more than 1,000 CEOs, CFOs and other top corporate officers. It was a former managing partner there, Ron Daniel, who [coined](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-art-of-21st-century-leadership-from-succession-planning-to-building-a-leadership-factory "Link to McKinsey paper") the term “leadership factory” back in the 1980s.

The common knock on consultants as overly analytical PowerPoint jockeys persists in some boardrooms. One longtime CEO headhunter who requested anonymity to protect client relationships said some boards still give strict instructions in CEO searches: *No Consultants!*

Their concerns aren’t just theoretical. While consultants may look great on paper, they can suffer from analysis paralysis — overthinking a problem and delaying a key decision. “There's a big difference between being smart and being decisive,” ghSMART’s Botelho said. “Decisiveness is a factor of speed and the quality of your decision making. We do see \[former\] consultants sometimes struggle with that.”

Sometimes the struggles are more idiosyncratic. Take Ashley Buchanan, who went to Accenture as a newly minted MBA before launching a career in retail. He just got [fired](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-01/kohl-s-terminates-ceo-for-violating-policy-names-replacement?sref=QnKyEnuc "Link to story") as CEO of Kohl’s Corp. after an internal [probe](https://investors.kohls.com/news/news-details/2025/Kohls-Announces-CEO-Transition-Process/default.aspx "Link to statement") uncovered an undisclosed personal relationship with a vendor to whom he steered millions of dollars of business.

Perhaps more troubling than any individual failure, though, is the spate of consulting industry [scandals](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2023-11-22/mckinsey-and-its-consulting-industry-peers-face-the-wildest-headwinds-in-years) involving ethical lapses, dodgy clients or the lax oversight inherent in the partnership structures of most big consulting firms.

While it denied any conflict of interest, McKinsey advised Purdue Pharma on how to “ [turbocharge](https://www.mass.gov/doc/massachusetts-mckinsey-complaint/download) ” sales of OxyContin while [simultaneously working](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-04-27/mckinsey-chief-says-firm-failed-to-identify-opioid-epidemic) for the US Food and Drug Administration. The Australian arm of PwC [leaked](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-05-25/pwc-australia-tax-leak-scandal-what-happened-and-why-tom-seymour-stepped-down) confidential government tax plans to clients. EY’s German unit [failed](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-04-03/ey-germany-handed-two-year-ban-after-wirecard-audit-failures) to uncover rampant fraud at Wirecard AG, and Bain & Co. got [banned](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-09-29/s-african-treasury-bans-bain-from-public-contracts-for-10-years) from doing business with the UK and South African governments following a corruption scandal. These embarrassing episodes besmirched an industry that sells itself on upstanding culture and character.

Meanwhile, terminations of consulting [contracts](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2025-03-27/government-contracting-is-an-easy-target-for-doge-but-it-s-elusive) with the US government are creating new concerns about the sector’s future.

“The luster is a bit off the consulting firms today,” Botelho said. But the industry’s pipeline of CEO talent looks sturdy enough now to weather it.
